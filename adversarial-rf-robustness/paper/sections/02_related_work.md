# 2. Related Work

## 2.1 Deep Learning for RF Modulation Classification

The application of deep learning to automatic modulation classification (AMC) was catalyzed by O'Shea et al. [1], who demonstrated that convolutional neural networks (CNNs) operating directly on raw I/Q samples could achieve competitive recognition rates without hand-crafted features. Their introduction of the RadioML 2016.10a dataset established a common benchmark that has been widely adopted for subsequent work. Since then, a variety of architectures have been explored: CLDNN models [3] combine convolutional feature extraction with LSTM temporal modeling to improve low-SNR performance, while systematic architecture searches [4] have evaluated CNN, ResNet, and DenseNet variants for modulation classification. Deep residual networks applied to the larger RadioML 2018.01a dataset [2] achieve high accuracy at high SNR but, like all deep architectures, rely on learned representations whose adversarial robustness has not been systematically characterized.

A key observation across this body of work is that classification accuracy degrades substantially at low SNR, and that performance is sensitive to the channel conditions under which models are trained and evaluated [19]. Models trained on clean or AWGN-only data often generalize poorly to fading environments, motivating channel-aware training strategies.

## 2.2 Adversarial Machine Learning

Adversarial vulnerability in deep neural networks was formalized by Goodfellow et al. [6], who introduced the Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) and showed that linear properties of high-dimensional classifiers make them susceptible to small, carefully crafted perturbations. Madry et al. [7] strengthened this line of work by framing adversarial robustness as a min-max optimization problem and introducing Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) as a strong first-order attack. PGD-based adversarial training remains the most widely adopted empirical defense. Carlini and Wagner [8] further demonstrated that optimization-based attacks can defeat many proposed defenses, establishing rigorous evaluation standards for robustness claims.

On the defense side, Cohen et al. [13] introduced randomized smoothing as a method for providing certified robustness guarantees by averaging predictions over Gaussian-perturbed inputs. This approach is architecture-agnostic and provides provable L2 robustness certificates, making it a natural candidate for adaptation to the RF domain where additive noise is physically meaningful. Extensions to other norm balls [14] and practical adversarial training variants that reduce computational cost [15] have broadened the defense toolkit.

## 2.3 Adversarial Attacks on RF Classifiers

Several groups have applied adversarial attack techniques specifically to RF signal classifiers. Flowers et al. [9] demonstrated that FGSM, PGD, and momentum-based iterative attacks can substantially degrade modulation classification accuracy under L-inf perturbation constraints. However, those results used norm-based perturbation bounds (L-inf) at perturbation magnitudes that correspond to substantially higher perturbation-to-signal ratios than the norm-ratio constraints (||δ||_2 / ||x||_2 ≤ ρ) grounded in RF physics that we employ; the constraint type difference means reported perturbation magnitudes and attack success rates are not directly comparable across studies. Additionally, evaluation conditions in that work did not explicitly control the interaction between native dataset SNR labels and evaluation-time noise injection. As we show in Section 5, adopting physically grounded norm-ratio constraints and correcting for this evaluation artifact substantially changes the quantitative picture. Targeted attacks [10] have shown that adversarial perturbations transfer across architectures with non-trivial success rates (30-70%), indicating that the vulnerability is not architecture-specific but reflects fundamental properties of learned RF representations.

More recent work has explored physically motivated attack variants. Frequency-selective adversarial perturbations [11] operate in the spectral domain and can be designed to be stealthy in power spectral density, while studies on GaN power amplifier identification [12] have demonstrated that RF fingerprinting systems are similarly vulnerable. Detection-based defenses [17] using peak-to-average power ratio (PAPR) analysis have shown promise but rely on statistical properties that sophisticated attackers may circumvent.

A critical finding across this literature is that adversarial perturbations generated in the digital domain often degrade when transmitted over the air, due to the corrupting effect of channel propagation on the perturbation structure [17, 23]. This observation motivates channel-aware attack design and raises the question of whether channel impairments provide accidental robustness -- a question our experiments directly address. We find that fading channels provide substantial accidental robustness, not by attenuating the perturbation per se, but by degrading classifier accuracy to the point where the adversarial gradient becomes decorrelated from the received signal geometry.

## 2.4 Channel Effects and Adversarial Robustness

The interaction between wireless channel impairments and adversarial robustness remains poorly understood. RF transceivers contend with a range of impairments including AWGN, multipath fading (Rayleigh and Rician), carrier frequency offset, phase noise, and timing offsets [18]. These impairments degrade classifier performance even in the absence of adversarial manipulation [19], and their interaction with intentional perturbations is complex: channel effects may attenuate adversarial perturbations (reducing attack effectiveness) or compound with them (amplifying classifier degradation).

Channel-aware adversarial attacks [21] that account for expected channel response have been proposed to generate perturbations that survive propagation, while over-the-air (OTA) adversarial attack frameworks [23] have demonstrated that physically realizable attacks require fundamentally different design considerations than digital-domain perturbations. On the defense side, training with simulated channel augmentation [22] has been shown to improve both clean accuracy under impairments and adversarial robustness, leveraging the RF environment itself as a regularization mechanism.

However, no prior work has systematically evaluated adversarial robustness across a controlled matrix of channel conditions (AWGN, fading, CFO), perturbation budgets, and defense strategies while reporting the computational tradeoffs critical for edge-deployed systems. This gap is particularly significant for electronic warfare applications where classifiers must operate reliably under contested spectrum conditions.

## 2.5 Electronic Warfare and Cognitive Spectrum Operations

AI-enabled spectrum operations are a strategic priority for military organizations. Cognitive electronic warfare (CEW) leverages machine learning to achieve machine-speed spectrum awareness, adaptive jamming, and rapid signal identification [24]. The U.S. Department of Defense has invested in AI/ML-driven spectrum management through programs including DARPA's RF Machine Learning Systems initiative [26], which aims to develop goal-driven RF systems capable of learning from data and adapting to dynamic threats [25].

In this context, adversarial vulnerability of RF classifiers represents a direct operational risk: misclassified waveforms can trigger incorrect countermeasures, degrade spectrum situational awareness, and enable adversary deception. The robustness of AI-based RF classifiers is therefore not merely an academic concern but a system requirement for fielded cognitive EW capabilities. Yet the EW community has largely treated adversarial robustness as a peripheral concern rather than a first-order design constraint, and there is limited work bridging the adversarial ML and EW communities with operationally grounded experimental evaluations.

## 2.6 Positioning of This Work

Our work addresses the gap at the intersection of adversarial machine learning and RF signal classification under realistic channel conditions. Unlike prior RF adversarial studies that evaluate attacks in isolation [9, 10] or under single channel conditions, we systematically vary both channel impairments and perturbation budgets across an SNR sweep on two benchmark datasets of increasing complexity (RadioML 2016.10a and 2018.01a), disentangling the contributions of adversarial manipulation and channel degradation and characterizing how adversarial vulnerability scales with task complexity. Critically, we identify and correct a potential evaluation pitfall -- the double application of AWGN noise to samples that already contain noise at their labeled SNR -- which can inflate reported attack success rates when using pre-collected benchmark datasets. Unlike certified robustness work developed for computer vision [13, 14], we ground our threat model in RF physics using L2 norm-ratio constraints (||δ||_2 / ||x||_2 ≤ ρ, where ρ is a norm ratio and power ratio = ρ²) and evaluate under propagation conditions relevant to contested spectrum operations. We assume white-box attacks with no channel state information (no-CSI), representing the strongest attacker without access to unknown propagation effects. And unlike EW doctrine discussions [24, 25] that identify AI robustness as a need, we provide quantitative experimental evidence showing that channel impairments -- not adversarial perturbations -- are the dominant source of classifier degradation at operationally relevant power levels, that adversarial vulnerability scales significantly with the number of modulation classes (from 7-9% ASR on 11 classes to 31-35% on 24 classes), and we offer actionable defense comparisons with computational overhead analysis suitable for edge deployment.
